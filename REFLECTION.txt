AI-ASSISTED GAME DEVELOPMENT REFLECTION

WHERE AI EXCELLED

AI assistance proved exceptionally valuable in three key areas during development. First, rapid prototyping and boilerplate generation allowed me to quickly establish foundational systems like the lane movement architecture, collectible spawning logic, and HUD framework. What might have taken hours to scaffold manually was accomplished in minutes, freeing time for creative decisions and refinement.

Second, AI excelled at systematic debugging when I encountered the collectible pickup issue where coins triggered from 19+ units away. The AI suggested comprehensive logging strategies that revealed oversized mesh colliders on imported prefabs. This diagnostic approach - adding strategic debug outputs, analyzing patterns, then implementing targeted fixes - proved more effective than trial-and-error debugging.

Third, documentation generation was significantly accelerated. Creating the README, implementation summary, and requirements documentation would typically consume several hours post-development. AI assistance structured these documents professionally while I focused on accuracy verification and adding personal insights.


HOW AI ALTERED MY TECHNICAL AND CREATIVE PROCESS

Technical Process Changes:

My traditional development approach involves extensive upfront planning, manual code writing, and incremental testing. AI collaboration fundamentally shifted this to a more iterative, conversational workflow. Instead of spending hours researching Unity collision systems or Timer.deltaTime vs Time.unscaledDeltaTime behavior, I could describe problems in natural language and receive contextually relevant solutions.

However, this efficiency came with a critical learning: I could no longer trust implementations blindly. Early in development, I accepted an AI suggestion to use proximity-based pickup with a 0.7-unit radius. This seemed reasonable in isolation but caused phantom pickups when combined with oversized colliders. I learned to treat AI outputs as "confident first drafts" requiring validation rather than authoritative solutions.

The debugging cycle evolved into a collaborative dialogue. When coins disappeared microseconds after spawning, I described the behavior, the AI suggested potential causes (self-collection, tile cleanup, overlapping colliders), and we systematically eliminated possibilities. This partnership accelerated problem-solving but required me to maintain critical oversight.

Creative Process Changes:

AI influenced my creative process more subtly. When designing the HUD layout, I initially considered a minimalist approach with only essential elements. AI suggested the comprehensive six-element HUD design based on the design document's survival pressure theme, recommending color-coded timer states, momentary alert feeds, and auto-hiding control hints. This expanded my creative thinking beyond my initial conservative approach.

However, I consciously resisted AI's tendency toward feature bloat. When AI suggested power-ups, combo multipliers, and particle effects, I evaluated each against the three-day timeline and core design goals. This taught me to use AI as a creative catalyst while maintaining disciplined scope management.

The most significant creative impact was in problem-solving approaches. Rather than abandoning proximity pickup when it initially failed, AI suggested keeping the feature but fixing the root cause (oversized colliders). This "fix the foundation, not remove the feature" mindset proved valuable and will influence future design decisions.


WHAT I WOULD CHANGE ABOUT AI COLLABORATION

Understanding Over Implementation:

My biggest mistake was accepting code without fully understanding the underlying systems. When implementing the collectible parent-to-tile cleanup, I copied the raycast logic without fully comprehending Unity's transform hierarchy and parenting behavior. This caused confusion later when debugging despawn timing. Future collaboration must prioritize explanation alongside implementation - I should request "explain why this works" before accepting solutions.

Iterative Scope Definition:

I initially provided broad requests like "implement a collectible system," which led to over-engineered solutions with proximity pickup, spawn protection, lifetime limits, and multiple fallback mechanisms. More effective would be: "implement basic trigger-based pickup first, then we'll add complexity if needed." This incremental approach reduces debugging complexity and clarifies which features actually improve gameplay.

Documentation During Development:

All documentation was created post-implementation, requiring me to reconstruct decision rationale from memory and git history. A better approach would be maintaining a development journal throughout, documenting why specific changes were made when they occurred. AI could assist by suggesting journal entries after significant decisions.

Testing Validation:

I relied too heavily on AI's assertion that solutions "should work" without systematic testing. When proximity pickup failed multiple times, I kept trying variations rather than stepping back to validate fundamental assumptions (collider sizes, layer settings, trigger configurations). Future collaboration should include explicit testing checkpoints before proceeding to next features.


ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS

Attribution and Transparency:

Throughout development, AI generated approximately 60-70% of the initial code structures, though nearly all required significant modification through iterative refinement. The ethical question becomes: when does AI assistance constitute co-authorship versus tool usage? I've credited AI through git commit co-authorship and explicit acknowledgment in documentation, treating it as a collaborative partner rather than invisible tool.

This raises authenticity concerns for academic submission. The code is technically "mine" in that I directed all implementation decisions, validated correctness, and debugged issues. However, I did not personally type most lines of code. I believe this relationship is analogous to using a code generation tool or framework, but remains ethically ambiguous in academic contexts where individual technical competency is assessed.

Learning vs Dependency:

A concerning pattern emerged during development: I became dependent on AI for syntax I should have learned independently. When implementing Unity UI anchors and Canvas Scaler configuration, I asked AI for the solution rather than consulting Unity documentation. This creates a knowledge gap - I can ship features quickly but may not deeply understand systems I've "implemented."

This dependency risks producing developers who can direct AI but cannot code independently. For future projects, I would mandate independent research for fundamental concepts before requesting AI assistance, using AI for acceleration rather than substitution of learning.

Asset Generation and Ownership:

The design document, game concept, and all creative direction originated from my own ideation. AI did not generate game mechanics, narrative concepts, or aesthetic decisions. However, AI did influence implementation approaches and suggested specific technical patterns. The creative ownership is clear, but technical authorship is collaborative.

All 3D models, textures, and audio assets came from Unity Asset Store or Mixamo with proper licenses. No AI-generated visual or audio assets were used, avoiding copyright ambiguity. Future projects using AI image/audio generation would require careful consideration of attribution and licensing.


DID YOU CREDIT AI-GENERATED ASSETS APPROPRIATELY?

Yes, with multi-layered attribution:

1. Git Commits: All commits include "Co-authored-by: Cursor" trailer acknowledging AI contribution to implementation
2. README Credits: Explicitly states "AI Assistance: Code generation, debugging, system design consultation"
3. Design Document: Introduction acknowledges AI was used for code generation, refining, and debugging
4. This Reflection: Comprehensive discussion of AI's role and impact

However, the granularity of attribution remains challenging. Some scripts like LaneMath.cs are nearly 100% AI-generated with minimal modification. Others like HUDController.cs underwent 10+ iterations with substantial human direction and restructuring. Current attribution doesn't differentiate between these levels of AI contribution.

A more honest approach might include per-file attribution comments indicating AI contribution percentage, but this risks over-complicating documentation and may not be standard practice in industry settings where AI tools are increasingly normalized.


WHERE RELYING ON AI FELT ETHICALLY QUESTIONABLE

The Collision System Debugging Cycle:

The most ethically uncomfortable moment occurred during the 4-hour debugging session for collectible pickups. After multiple failed AI solutions (proximity radius tuning, spawn delay adjustments, collision layer changes), I felt increasingly disconnected from understanding the actual problem. I was implementing suggestions without comprehension, essentially performing mechanical trial-and-error directed by AI.

This felt ethically questionable for academic assessment because my problem-solving ability was not genuinely demonstrated - I was a conduit for AI's debugging process rather than an independent developer. The eventual solution (disabling prefab colliders and replacing with controlled triggers) came from AI pattern recognition across many failed attempts, not my technical understanding.

For academic integrity, this raises the question: if I cannot reproduce the solution methodology independently, do I truly understand the system? The answer was no at that moment, though I've since studied Unity collision systems to fill that knowledge gap.

Documentation Authenticity:

When generating the implementation summary and README, AI produced professional-quality documentation exceeding what I would typically write independently. The structure, technical depth, and presentation quality are clearly AI-enhanced. While I reviewed and approved all content, the writing voice is not authentically mine.

This creates a false impression of my documentation skill level. Future employers or educators reading these documents would assume a writing capability I don't naturally possess. For academic honesty, this reflection serves as disclosure that documentation was AI-assisted.

Decision Validation:

Several times I accepted AI recommendations without fully evaluating alternatives. For example, using FindFirstObjectByType for HUD references is functional but not optimal for performance. AI suggested it, I implemented it, but I didn't independently research whether UnityEvents, singleton patterns, or dependency injection would be more appropriate.

This passive acceptance of AI expertise felt ethically questionable because it represented minimal independent critical thinking. I was optimizing for speed over learning, which may be acceptable professionally but questionable in educational contexts emphasizing skill development.


HOW TO ENSURE FUTURE AI WORK REMAINS RESPONSIBLE AND AUTHENTIC

Establish Clear Boundaries:

Future projects will define explicit "AI-free zones" where I must implement solutions independently to demonstrate core competency. For example: basic movement controllers, fundamental collision detection, or essential game loops must be attempted manually before AI assistance. This ensures baseline technical capability remains sharp.

Mandatory Understanding Checkpoints:

Before accepting any AI-generated solution, I will implement a "explain it back" protocol - I must be able to articulate why the solution works, what alternatives exist, and what trade-offs were made. If I cannot explain it to another developer, I do not sufficiently understand it and should not use it.

Transparent Attribution Framework:

Future documentation will include:
1. Development methodology statement disclosing AI tool usage
2. Per-component attribution in code comments (e.g., "// AI-generated, modified for X")
3. Clear distinction between AI-suggested architecture vs AI-written implementation
4. Honest assessment of which skills were demonstrated vs delegated

This transparency allows fair assessment of my actual capabilities while acknowledging AI's contribution.

Progressive Responsibility Model:

I will implement a graduated approach to AI assistance:
- Phase 1: Attempt solution independently (30 min minimum)
- Phase 2: If stuck, research documentation/tutorials (30 min)
- Phase 3: Only then request AI assistance with specific context
- Phase 4: Validate and modify AI solution with full understanding

This ensures AI accelerates rather than replaces learning, maintaining authentic skill development.

Version Control Discipline:

Future git commits will be more granular, showing iterative development rather than large feature dumps. This creates an honest record of the development process, including failed attempts and refinements, rather than a polished false narrative of smooth AI-assisted development.

Ethical Review Questions:

Before using AI for any project component, I will ask:
1. Is this task something I should be able to do independently?
2. Am I using AI to learn faster or to avoid learning?
3. Will this create a false impression of my capabilities?
4. Can I maintain and modify this code six months from now?
5. Am I being transparent about AI's role with stakeholders?

If answers reveal concerning dependencies or misrepresentation risks, I will adjust my approach.


CONCLUSION

This game jam demonstrated both the tremendous productivity potential and ethical complexity of AI-assisted development. AI excelled at acceleration, pattern recognition, and documentation structure, but required constant critical oversight to prevent knowledge gaps and ensure authentic learning.

The technical outcome is successful - a functional game with documented systems. However, the learning outcome is mixed: some areas (collision system architecture, UI management, audio integration) I now understand deeply through debugging and iteration. Other areas (some Unity lifecycle nuances, optimal design patterns) I implemented without full comprehension.

Future AI collaboration must balance efficiency with authentic learning, transparency with practical development needs, and speed with genuine skill development. The goal is not to reject AI assistance but to use it responsibly as a learning accelerator rather than a learning replacement, ensuring both project success and personal growth as a developer.
